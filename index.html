A Quick Look Into Russian Twitter Trolling During the 2016 Election

Brief:

On 02.14.18 NBC News released a story (NBC Exposé) indicating the level and scale of Russian meddling in the USA 2016 Presidential election. Included in NBC's story were several CSV files full of Russian identified troll accounts that Twitter has retroactively deleted. Regardless of your political leanings it is well established fact at this point that the meddling both A: occurred, and B: was conducted by Russians (less concretely: with the Russian government's support and consent). This tactic has also occurred during the democratic processes of Western European nations (USA Today List). There is, to an extent (in my own view), an uncanny irony in that we were targeted so easily by foreigners using communications technologies that we invented.

What's This Page For?:

I created this page for a couple of reasons, one being that my original studies involved International Relations (a mixture of Political Science, Economics, and Geography), I earned a BA in IR at Kent State University. Now that I'm moving in the direction of a career in programming/web development I found the opportunity of parsing through a large dataset of evidence from a political hack simply too good to pass up.

The other reason is that I wanted to 'get something out there' demonstrating my coding ability while I work on getting my other 'true' full-stack DDWA projects live and online.

Methodology: I downloaded NBC's CSVs for data scientists from their story, parsed the CSVs to JSON arrays consisting of both an array of Twitter account objects and one consisting of Twitter post objects associated with Twitter identified Russian troll accounts. I then began extracting the data using AJAX calls locally against the parsed JSON files to 'see what I could glean'. The charts are made possible by a great JS CDN called Plot.ly. The source code for this project is located on my Github page (2016ElectionsShenanigans).

How To Use This?:

The volume of posts that NBC captured is large (over 200k); thus, though the data is iterated on the client side efficiently enough, displaying individual posts would freeze your browser. For the sake of transparency, or if you want to check me, you can get the JSONs on my Github page (above) or grab them from your browser's devtools.

This page is not formatted for nor meant for display on mobile devices; in addition, the content will not display on Chrome, use Firefox or Edge instead as alternatives for now.
